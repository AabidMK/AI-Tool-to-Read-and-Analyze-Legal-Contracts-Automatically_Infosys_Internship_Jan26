CONTRACT REVIEW REPORT
==================================================
Contract Type: Data Protection Agreement
Industry: Technology

1. CLAUSE ANALYSIS
------------------------------
Roles of the Parties: Present
Processing of Personal Data (Cisco's undertaking to process only on instructions): Weak

Missing Clauses:
- Confidentiality of Processing (for personnel)
- Security Measures (Cisco's explicit obligation to implement and maintain appropriate technical and organizational measures)
- Conditions for Sub-processing (beyond definition, e.g., Customer authorization, flow-down obligations, notification mechanism, liability for sub-processors)
- Assistance to Controller (for Data Subject Rights requests)
- Data Breach Notification and Assistance (Cisco's obligations to notify, provide details, and assist the Customer)
- Data Return and Deletion (obligations upon termination or expiry of the Agreement)
- Audits and Inspections (Customer's right to audit Processor's compliance)
- International Data Transfers (mechanisms for lawful transfers, e.g., SCCs)
- General Assistance to Controller (e.g., for Data Protection Impact Assessments (DPIAs) and consultations with supervisory authorities)

General Suggestions:
- **Strengthen 'Processing of Personal Data' clause:** Add an explicit undertaking by Cisco that it shall process Personal Data only on documented instructions from the Customer, as set out in this DPA and the Agreement, and in compliance with Data Protection Laws. This should be a direct obligation on Cisco, not merely implied from Customer's responsibilities.
- **Elaborate on 'Scope of Processing':** While Section 2.2 defines duration and purpose, consider adding a clause or an appendix (if not already covered by Privacy Data Sheets/Offer Disclosures in sufficient detail) that explicitly lists the types of Personal Data processed, the categories of Data Subjects, and the nature and specific purposes of the processing activities. Clarify that Privacy Data Sheets/Offer Disclosures are integral to the DPA and govern these specifics.
- **Add a 'Confidentiality' clause:** Include a clause requiring Cisco to ensure that persons authorized to process Personal Data are bound by appropriate confidentiality obligations.
- **Add an explicit 'Security Measures' clause:** While the 'Information Security Exhibit' is referenced, the DPA should contain a direct contractual obligation for Cisco to implement and maintain appropriate technical and organizational measures to protect Personal Data.
- **Detail 'Sub-processing' conditions:** Expand on Section 1.19 (Subprocessor definition) to include: (a) the requirement for Customer's prior specific or general written authorization for engaging Subprocessors; (b) Cisco's obligation to impose equivalent data protection obligations on Subprocessors; (c) a mechanism for notifying the Customer of new Subprocessors and allowing the Customer to object; and (d) Cisco's liability for the acts and omissions of its Subprocessors.
- **Add 'Assistance to Controller' clauses:** Include separate clauses detailing Cisco's obligation to assist the Customer in responding to requests from Data Subjects exercising their rights, and to assist with Data Protection Impact Assessments (DPIAs) and prior consultations with supervisory authorities, where required.
- **Add a 'Data Breach Notification' clause:** Detail Cisco's obligation to notify the Customer without undue delay upon becoming aware of a Data Breach, including the information to be provided (e.g., nature of breach, categories of data, affected data subjects, measures taken/proposed) and assistance to the Customer.
- **Add a 'Data Return and Deletion' clause:** Specify Cisco's obligations regarding the return or deletion of Personal Data upon the termination or expiry of the Agreement.
- **Add an 'Audits and Inspections' clause:** Grant the Customer the right to conduct or mandate audits and inspections to verify Cisco's compliance with the DPA.

2. ROLE-BASED LEGAL REVIEWS
------------------------------

Role: Data Privacy Counsel Review
As Data Privacy Counsel, my review of the provided DPA text identifies a generally standard structure and intent to establish Cisco as a Processor and the Customer as a Controller. The definitions for CCPA and Cisco Offer are appropriately broad. However, a significant concern arises within the definition of 'Affiliates.' The carve-out for 'Acquired Entities' creates a potential loophole where entities within the Cisco group that process Customer's Personal Data might not be subject to the DPA's protections if their offers are not on an applicable Price List. This could lead to a situation where Personal Data is processed without the necessary contractual safeguards, posing a material risk to data subjects and the Controller's compliance obligations. The DPA should ensure that all entities processing Personal Data on behalf of the Processor (Cisco) are bound by its terms, whether directly as an 'Affiliate' or as a sub-processor. The current wording could allow for a gap in accountability and protection.
Suggested Modifications:
- Original: 1.1. “Affiliates” means, any corporation or company that directly or indirectly controls, or is controlled by, or is under common control with the relevant party, where “control” means to: (a) own over 50% of the relevant party; or (b) be able to direct the affairs of the relevant party through voting rights or other lawful means (e.g., a contract that allows control). Unless otherwise explicitly agreed by the Parties, any legal entities which become part of the Cisco group of companies through an acquisition or merger (each an “Acquired Entity”) are not considered Cisco Affiliates for the purposes of this DPA unless the Cisco Offers of such Acquired Entity are available for You to purchase on the applicable Price List.
  Suggested: 1.1. “Affiliates” means, any corporation or company that directly or indirectly controls, or is controlled by, or is under common control with the relevant party, where “control” means to: (a) own over 50% of the relevant party; or (b) be able to direct the affairs of the relevant party through voting rights or other lawful means (e.g., a contract that allows control).
  Reason: The original text includes a carve-out stating that 'Acquired Entities' are not considered Cisco Affiliates for DPA purposes unless their offers are on an applicable Price List. This creates a significant data privacy risk. If an Acquired Entity processes Personal Data on behalf of the Customer (as Controller) in connection with Cisco Offers, it must be bound by the DPA's terms, regardless of its commercial status or whether its specific offers are on a price list. The DPA's protections should extend to all entities within the Processor's group that handle Personal Data. Removing this carve-out ensures that any entity meeting the general 'control' definition is considered an Affiliate and thus subject to the DPA's obligations when processing Personal Data, or is otherwise treated as a sub-processor under the DPA.

Role: Information Security Legal Specialist Review
As an Information Security Legal Specialist, my review of the provided Data Protection Agreement (DPA) text identifies several standard and generally acceptable clauses, but also a critical area of concern regarding the definition of 'Affiliates'.

**Strengths:**
*   **Clear Scope and Application:** The DPA clearly defines its applicability where Cisco acts as a Processor for Customer's Personal Data, forming part of the main Agreement.
*   **Supersession Clause:** The clause stating that this DPA supersedes previous privacy or data protection agreements is crucial for legal clarity and avoiding conflicting obligations.
*   **Comprehensive CCPA Definition:** The definition of 'CCPA' includes amendments by CPRA and related regulations/guidance, demonstrating an awareness of evolving privacy legislation.

**Areas for Improvement/Concern:**
*   **Future Template Date:** The 'Template date: September 2025' is in the future, which is likely a typographical error and should be corrected to reflect an actual past or present date.
*   **Critical Issue: Affiliate Definition (Section 1.1):** The most significant concern lies within the definition of 'Affiliates'. The exclusion of 'Acquired Entities' from being considered Cisco Affiliates for DPA purposes, 'unless the Cisco Offers of such Acquired Entity are available for You to purchase on the applicable Price List,' creates a substantial loophole. From a data protection perspective, if an acquired entity processes the Customer's Personal Data, it acts as a sub-processor or part of the Processor's group. The Controller (Customer) requires assurance that all entities handling its data are bound by the DPA's obligations (e.g., security measures, audit rights, data subject rights support, breach notification). Tying DPA applicability to commercial availability on a price list is irrelevant to data protection compliance and introduces a significant risk that Customer data could be processed by an entity not subject to the DPA's protective terms. This could lead to a breach of the Controller's obligations under data protection laws (e.g., GDPR Article 28, CCPA/CPRA requirements for service providers).
*   **Incomplete Text:** The provided text cuts off at '1.6. Controll', indicating that crucial definitions such as 'Controller', 'Processor', 'Personal Data', 'Processing', and potentially others are missing. A full review would require these definitions to ensure alignment with applicable data protection laws and clarity of roles and responsibilities.

**Overall Assessment:**
While the DPA establishes a foundational framework, the specific carve-out in the 'Affiliates' definition presents a material risk to the Controller's data protection compliance. This clause must be modified to ensure comprehensive coverage for all entities processing Customer Personal Data under the Agreement. The future template date is a minor administrative correction.
Suggested Modifications:
- Original: Template date: September 2025
  Suggested: Template date: [Current or Actual Template Date, e.g., September 2023]
  Reason: The template date is in the future, which is likely a typographical error. It should reflect the actual date the template was created or last updated for accuracy.
- Original: Unless otherwise explicitly agreed by the Parties, any legal entities which become part of the Cisco group of companies through an acquisition or merger (each an “Acquired Entity”) are not considered Cisco Affiliates for the purposes of this DPA unless the Cisco Offers of such Acquired Entity are available for You to purchase on the applicable Price List.
  Suggested: Unless otherwise explicitly agreed by the Parties, any legal entities which become part of the Cisco group of companies through an acquisition or merger (each an “Acquired Entity”) are considered Cisco Affiliates for the purposes of this DPA if they Process Personal Data on behalf of You under the Agreement.
  Reason: The original clause creates a significant loophole where an acquired entity processing Customer Personal Data might not be bound by the DPA's terms if its offers are not on a Cisco price list. This is a critical data protection risk for the Controller. Data protection obligations should apply to any entity processing the Controller's data, regardless of internal commercial arrangements or product availability. The suggested modification ensures that all entities within the Processor's group that handle Customer Personal Data are subject to the DPA's requirements, aligning with the Controller's need for comprehensive data protection assurances and compliance with laws like GDPR and CCPA/CPRA.

Role: International Data Transfer Counsel Review
As International Data Transfer Counsel, my review of the provided partial Data Protection Agreement (DPA) text reveals critical deficiencies regarding international data transfers. While the document correctly establishes the Controller-Processor relationship and defines key terms like CCPA, it is critically silent on the mechanisms and safeguards required for cross-border data transfers, which is a fundamental requirement under global data protection laws.

Specifically:
1.  **Absence of International Transfer Mechanisms:** The DPA lacks any provisions addressing transfers of Personal Data outside the European Economic Area (EEA), the UK, or other jurisdictions with specific data export requirements (e.g., under GDPR Chapter V or UK GDPR). There is no mention of Standard Contractual Clauses (SCCs) (EU or UK), Binding Corporate Rules (BCRs), or other approved transfer tools. This omission represents a significant legal and compliance risk, particularly in light of the Schrems II ruling and subsequent guidance requiring robust transfer impact assessments and supplementary measures.
2.  **Ambiguity in Affiliate Scope for Data Processing:** The definition of 'Affiliates' (Section 1.1) contains a carve-out for 'Acquired Entities' unless their offers are on the applicable Price List. This creates a potential loophole where an entity within the Cisco group that processes Personal Data on behalf of the Customer might not be contractually bound by the DPA's terms if it falls outside this narrow definition. If such an entity is located in a third country, this could lead to inadequate contractual coverage for data protection obligations and international transfer safeguards.
3.  **Incomplete Document:** The provided text is incomplete, cutting off at 'Controll'. A full review would require the entire DPA to assess other critical aspects such as sub-processor management, data subject rights, security measures, and government access requests, all of which have implications for international data transfers.
Suggested Modifications:
- Original: (Absence of a dedicated clause for international data transfers)
  Suggested: Add a new section, for example, Section 2, titled "International Data Transfers."

"2. International Data Transfers
2.1. To the extent that Cisco Processes Personal Data originating from the European Economic Area (EEA) or the United Kingdom (UK) in a country outside the EEA or UK not deemed to provide an adequate level of data protection by the European Commission or the UK government (as applicable), the Parties agree that the EU Standard Contractual Clauses (Commission Implementing Decision (EU) 2021/914 of 4 June 2021) and, where applicable, the UK International Data Transfer Addendum to the EU Standard Contractual Clauses (version B1.0, in force 21 March 2022) or the UK International Data Transfer Agreement (IDTA), shall apply. The Parties agree to complete and execute such clauses as necessary, with You acting as the data exporter and Cisco as the data importer.
2.2. The Parties acknowledge that transfers under the Standard Contractual Clauses or IDTA may require supplementary measures to ensure a level of protection essentially equivalent to that guaranteed within the EEA or UK. Cisco shall implement appropriate technical and organizational measures to ensure such protection, taking into account the nature, scope, context, and purposes of processing, as well as the risks of varying likelihood and severity for the rights and freedoms of natural persons.
2.3. For transfers of Personal Data from other jurisdictions with specific cross-border transfer requirements, the Parties shall cooperate in good faith to implement appropriate transfer mechanisms as required by applicable Data Protection Laws."
  Reason: This DPA is critically missing provisions for international data transfers, which are mandatory under GDPR (Chapter V), UK GDPR, and other global data protection laws. The inclusion of a dedicated clause referencing the applicable Standard Contractual Clauses (EU SCCs and UK Addendum/IDTA) and acknowledging the need for supplementary measures is essential to ensure legal compliance and mitigate the significant risks associated with cross-border data flows, especially post-Schrems II.
- Original: Unless otherwise explicitly agreed by the Parties, any legal entities which become part of the Cisco group of companies through an acquisition or merger (each an “Acquired Entity”) are not considered Cisco Affiliates for the purposes of this DPA unless the Cisco Offers of such Acquired Entity are available for You to purchase on the applicable Price List.
  Suggested: Unless otherwise explicitly agreed by the Parties, any legal entities which become part of the Cisco group of companies through an acquisition or merger (each an “Acquired Entity”) are not considered Cisco Affiliates for the purposes of this DPA unless the Cisco Offers of such Acquired Entity are available for You to purchase on the applicable Price List. Notwithstanding the foregoing, any Acquired Entity that Processes Personal Data on behalf of Cisco in connection with the Cisco Offers shall be subject to the obligations of this DPA as if it were a Cisco Affiliate, and Cisco shall ensure that such Acquired Entity complies with these obligations, including any requirements for international data transfers and sub-processing.
  Reason: The current definition creates a potential gap in contractual coverage. If an Acquired Entity processes Personal Data on behalf of Cisco but is not considered an 'Affiliate' under this DPA's specific commercial definition, its processing activities might not be adequately governed by the DPA's data protection safeguards, including those related to international transfers. This modification ensures that all entities involved in processing Personal Data under the DPA are bound by its terms, thereby maintaining a consistent level of data protection and compliance, regardless of internal corporate structuring or commercial listing status.
